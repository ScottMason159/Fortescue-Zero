Major Hazard Control Flow

A practical guide for manufacturing teams learning how major hazards are identified, controlled, verified, actioned and assured.

Start with the hazard. Confirm the risk. Own the controls. Verify they work. Fix what fails. Review and improve.

Start learning Read executive summary

Simple overview

The process is a control health cycle.

The aim is not to create more paperwork. The aim is to make sure fatality-potential risks have clear controls, clear owners and clear evidence that the controls work.

  1. 1 Find hazards that could credibly cause fatal harm.
  2. 2 Define the material risk and the controls that matter most.
  3. 3 Verify critical controls against a clear standard.
  4. 4 Fix failed controls, review trends and improve the system.

Plain text fallback:
Start with new work, operation, project or change.
Define the scope, location, activity and assets.
Identify hazards and fatality risks.
Check whether Health and Safety MPC is Level 4 or higher.
If no, manage through the standard risk process.
If yes, confirm the major hazard and record the material risk if criteria are met.
Define the risk event, causes, consequences and ratings.
Appoint the Risk Owner.
Identify controls and decide which controls are critical.
Assign Control Owners for critical controls.
Document performance standards and testing requirements.
Create, schedule and complete CCVs.
If a CCV fails, raise an action, assign an owner, set evidence requirements and escalate if the risk is not controlled.
Close the action only when evidence is accepted.
Complete Control Effectiveness Tests and Material Risk Reviews.
Update the risk profile, controls and CCVs.
Repeat the assurance cycle.

Executive summary

The major hazard process helps the business identify fatality-potential risks, define the controls that matter most, verify those controls, fix failed controls and improve over time.

Manufacturing leaders need to own the risk, not just participate in the process.

Health and Safety can support the system, but line leaders must make sure controls are in place, people are competent, CCVs are completed, failed controls are actioned and overdue items are escalated.

The main implementation risk is complexity.

The process should be introduced using plain language, simple ownership, clear CCV rules, and a practical escalation model.
